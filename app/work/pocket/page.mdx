# Pocket

**The web home of Pocket, Mozilla's article-discovery service. A multi-surface editorial and personal-library platform that served more than 2 million daily page views at its peak. I was hired in 2017 as the frontend lead on Pocket's first rewrite; in 2020 I co-architected the second rewrite with one other engineer, Joel Kelly. I stayed through the Mozilla acquisition and the product's eventual shutdown, six years total.**

Shipped · [Source](https://github.com/Pocket/web-client)

## The Pitch

Pocket was the product people used to save articles they wanted to read later. The web client was the home surface for the service: a personalized discovery feed, a library of everything you'd saved, a reader that showed articles in Pocket's own formatting, a system of curated editorial slates and topic pages, shareable lists, highlights, text-to-speech narration, and a syndication layer where Pocket could publish full article text under license from partner publishers.

I joined Pocket as employee 24 in 2017, hired to build the first rewrite of the web application. I shipped that version and spent the following years extending it. In 2020, Joel Kelly and I rewrote the application again from scratch, this time in React, Next.js, Redux, and Apollo, and I co-architected that second generation end to end. I stayed at Pocket through the Mozilla acquisition and through Mozilla's decision in early 2024 to shut the product down. Six years, two codebases, one continuous arc as part of a small team building what was, for much of that time, one of the most widely-read surfaces in Mozilla's portfolio.

The codebase this case study discusses is the 2020 rewrite, which is the one still on GitHub. The repo serves a farewell page now; the pages that built the product are preserved in an `archive/` folder.

## The Interesting Problem

Publishing platforms look simple from the outside: fetch articles, render them, show a list. The actual interesting work is in the middle. Pocket served two fundamentally different kinds of content on the same site: personal, which is the user's private library of saved items, and editorial, which is the curated feed of recommendations Pocket's editors assembled for everyone. Both pulled from the same backend graph, both had to feel coherent in the same UI, and both had to perform at a scale where every millisecond of server render time and every kilobyte of client JavaScript had downstream consequences.

By 2020, the first rewrite had been in production for three years. It had grown the product and the team that built it. But we'd learned things. The original state architecture had been a pragmatic choice in 2017; by 2020 we knew how to do better. The editorial and personal surfaces had started to diverge in ways that strained the shared components. The analytics layer had been bolted on piece by piece and the result was instrumentation the business couldn't fully trust. The second rewrite was the chance to fix all of that with three years of hindsight.

The first architectural question Joel and I asked together was how to cache. Editorial slates updated multiple times a day; individual syndicated articles effectively never changed after publication; topic pages sat somewhere in between. The answer was a tiered Incremental Static Regeneration strategy: 60 seconds on the discovery home and topic pages, 24 hours on article pages, 12 hours on the not-found path for articles that had since been pulled. That's not a technical default. It's product thinking about content staleness, expressed as configuration.

The second architectural question was analytics. This is the piece I want to describe as my own work, because it is. Pocket ran experiments, surfaced recommendations, and made editorial decisions based on engagement data. If the analytics layer reports unreliable data, every downstream decision suffers, including the ones about which recommendations to surface, which editorial slates to build, and which features to invest in. Cleaning that up is the work I’m most proud of from my six years at Pocket.

The third question, present but not centered in this writeup, was the scope of the Redux architecture. Pocket's web client had a large surface area: personal library with bulk operations, collections, shareable lists, a reader with highlighting and font controls, editorial slates, topic pages, onboarding, search across both corpus and personal library, a text-to-speech subsystem, and full keyboard shortcuts. Redux and Redux-Saga held all of that together, with 401 action types and 47 saga watchers at its eventual size.

## Key Decisions and Tradeoffs

**Custom GraphQL client, not Apollo.** The web client calls a GraphQL backend for every piece of data it renders, but it does not use Apollo Client. All queries go through a hand-rolled fetch wrapper, and "deriver" functions normalize the GraphQL response into the flat, ID-keyed shapes Redux expects. The tradeoff is real: no reactive cache, no automatic subscription management, no refetch plumbing for free. The upside is that there's no dual state management problem where Apollo and Redux disagree about what's true, no cache invalidation logic we didn't write, and no second state layer to debug. For an application this large, where Redux was already the source of truth for everything else (including optimistic updates across a dozen mutation types), adding a second reactive cache would have duplicated infrastructure without a clear win. Joel and I made this call together and defended it across the lifetime of the rewrite.

**An analytics system with runtime type checking.** The one piece of architecture I most want to describe as my own is the Snowplow instrumentation layer. Pocket fired analytics events from nearly every interaction in the product. The naive implementation (dispatch an event, send its payload) fails silently when payloads are malformed, and malformed events are worse than no events: they produce incorrect dashboards and invalidate experiment results.

The system I built is declarative. Every event has a named definition (232 of them in the final state, organized into 20 surface-specific files) with an `expects` array that lists required payload fields. A runtime `validateSnowplowExpectations` function checks both presence and type against a 35-field type dictionary before every dispatch. Missing fields throw `SnowplowValueMissingError`; wrong types throw `SnowplowValueTypeError`. In development, violations print to the console. In production, they surface as Sentry errors rather than corrupting the analytics data silently.

This is what made the "70% reduction in instrumentation errors" I reported internally at Mozilla real rather than aspirational. The number came from Snowplow and Sentry dashboards comparing quarters before and after the rollout; the system underneath the number was a contract enforced at the point of dispatch, in TypeScript-flavored runtime code, across every event in a 50-reducer application.

**Impression deduplication at the reducer layer.** A subtle detail I want to surface because it was load-bearing for experiment quality: the impression tracker, which fires when a recommendation card enters the viewport, deduplicates per page view using a Set held in the Redux store and keyed by `impressionId || id || corpusRecommendationId || url`. The set is cleared on page-view transition. Without this, a user scrolling a feed would produce one impression event per card per scroll event, dwarfing the signal we cared about. With it, impressions mean what engagement analysts needed them to mean: one per recommendation per page view.

**A tiered ISR strategy for different content classes.** Discovery home and topic pages regenerate at most every 60 seconds because editorial slates update through the day. Individual syndicated article pages revalidate once per day because articles don't change after publication. Article-not-found responses revalidate every 12 hours because a not-found might represent an article still being processed by the syndication pipeline, and we didn't want to cache 404s as aggressively as we cached resolved pages. The missing-article path redirects to the original publisher URL with a 302. This is the cheapest possible expression of "the article isn't ours, but it exists out there; go read it."

**Performance: inline blocking script for color mode.** The `_document.js` injects a synchronous script before `<body>` that reads localStorage for the user's color mode preference and sets the appropriate CSS class on `<html>` before React hydrates. This is a standard pattern; including it here because it's one of those tiny pieces of engineering that fixes a flash-of-wrong-theme problem users would notice and never think to articulate. The same file wires the Snowplow tracker conditionally based on OneTrust cookie-consent state, with two separate tracker configurations depending on whether the user accepted analytics cookies.

**The two-rewrite arc.** The 2017 rewrite and the 2020 rewrite aren't the same story. In 2017 I was hired because Pocket knew its web experience needed to be modern and didn't have the team to build it themselves. I built the first version of the rewrite as the frontend lead on a small team, shipped it, and spent the next three years growing it with the team as Joel joined and the codebase became something we co-owned. In 2020, we decided we’d learned enough from the first version to do better, and rebuilt it from scratch, this time with Next.js, a proper state architecture, and the analytics layer built the way I now knew it needed to be built from the start. The six-year arc is not "I joined a project and stayed." It's "I was hired to build the first version, shipped it, grew it, and then with a trusted partner rebuilt it the way we should have built it the first time."

## Stack

Next.js 16 on the Pages Router. React. Redux with Redux-Saga for async and side-effect orchestration. GraphQL, accessed via a hand-rolled fetch client rather than Apollo. TypeScript and JavaScript in a mixed-migration pattern, with older modules in JS and newer additions in TS under an unenforced convention. pnpm workspaces with Turborepo task orchestration. Deployed to AWS ECS via CodeDeploy, not Vercel, with static assets served through S3 + CloudFront at `assets.getpocket.com`. Snowplow for analytics. Unleash for feature flags. next-i18next for localization across 14 locales. Mousetrap for keyboard shortcuts. react-modal with body-scroll-lock for dialog focus management. Libre Baskerville, OpenDyslexic, and seven other bundled fonts for reader typography.

## On the Analytics System

Worth a dedicated subsection because it's the single piece of this project I most want reviewers to look at. The system is at `clients/web/src/connectors/snowplow/` and the README there is the clearest artifact of the work: a 300-line developer guide covering initialization, event anatomy, how to read and add action definitions, how to test with Snowplow Micro and Mini, and how the OneTrust consent integration affects tracker configuration.

The architectural shape:

1. Events are organized into a three-layer hierarchy. Five event types (engagement, impression, content_open, object_update, variant_enroll); twelve entity types (api_user, content, corpus_recommendation, feature_flag, newsletter_subscriber, recommendation, report, shareable_list, shareable_list_item, slate, slate_lineup, ui); 232 named actions spread across 20 surface-specific files.
2. Each action has a definition: its event type, its required entities, and an `expects` array enumerating the payload fields it needs.
3. When a component dispatches `sendSnowplowEvent(identifier, data)`, a saga looks up the action, validates `data` against both the `expects` list and the type dictionary, and only then constructs the event and hands it to the Snowplow SDK.
4. Feature flag enrollment fires `variant_enroll` events automatically when flags hydrate, so experiment exposure is guaranteed to be tracked at the point a user becomes eligible.

This is not a glamorous piece of engineering. It is a durable one. The 70% error reduction I cite is the direct consequence of moving the cost of mistakes from "malformed production data" to "errors at the point of dispatch, caught in development or reported via Sentry in production." It also enabled the other claim on my resume that this case study doesn't dwell on: cutting time to statistical significance for Pocket's experiments, because the experiment framework downstream of this layer could finally trust that it had the signal it was promised.

## What I'd Do Differently

The repository shows a mixed-migration pattern between JavaScript and TypeScript. Older modules are JS, newer additions are TS, and there was no enforced convention to convert the older files on touch. The outcome is a codebase where the oldest and most critical code (parts of the Redux store, much of the deriver layer) has the weakest static typing. If I were doing it again, I would have burned a quarter on a full TS migration early, or at minimum required every touched file to be converted on modification. The cost of letting the migration drift showed up as small footguns over years.

Snapshot testing density in the component library is high, and snapshot tests tend to devolve into "approve every diff" rituals that don't catch real regressions. The test suite as a whole sits at roughly a 1:14 test-to-source ratio, which is sparse; the dense tests are concentrated in the derivers, where the payoff is highest, and the rest of the code depends heavily on integration-at-runtime. If I were rebuilding the test strategy I'd push harder on integration tests (Playwright, probably) and thin out snapshot density.

No accessibility testing in CI. The product had legitimate accessibility work (the keyboard shortcut system, OpenDyslexic as a reader option, OneTrust consent flows, modals with proper focus handling via react-modal), and I co-founded the accessibility working group at Pocket that produced the guidelines for color-blindness, screen readers, and keyboard navigation. But the CI pipeline gated tests only, not accessibility, and the working group's outputs were documents rather than automated tests. If I had one more quarter, jest-axe in the PR pipeline would have been the first addition.

## What I Learned Across Six Years and Two Rewrites

Two things. Maybe a third if I'm honest.

The first is that analytics is infrastructure. It is load-bearing for experiment validity, for product intuition, for every conversation with stakeholders who want to know whether a change worked. Treating analytics like a feature you add after the feature is done produces the dashboards that lie to you. Treating it like a contract, with validation at the point of dispatch, produces the dashboards you can trust. I now believe this deeply enough that I apply the same principle to analytics wherever I touch it.

The second is that the durable work on a long-lived codebase is often the work that nobody asks for. Nobody asked me to add runtime type checking to the analytics system. Nobody asked me to write a 300-line README for the Snowplow instrumentation layer. Nobody asked me to ship code generators for new Redux state slices so every new feature would scaffold the same way. The work that compounded over six years was the work Joel and I chose to do before anyone felt the pain.

The third, maybe, is the thing that only becomes visible when you build the same product twice. The 2017 rewrite shipped. The 2020 rewrite was better because we'd shipped the first one. The architectural choices I'm proudest of in the second version are the ones I'd have rolled my eyes at if someone had proposed them in 2017; I needed to have lived with the consequences of the first version's simpler choices to know why the more-considered choices were worth their complexity. There's no shortcut for that kind of knowledge. You earn it by building the thing, growing it, living with what doesn't work, and then earning the chance to build it again.

Pocket is shut down. This work ended. But the three things above are how I work now, on every codebase, and they will outlive the product they came from.
