# RO-SHAM-BO.EXE

**A retro-terminal rock-paper-scissors game that watches your patterns, learns your strategy, and remembers everything. None of that is true. The game is playing fair, the rest is performance.**

Active · [Live on the web](https://anthony-liddle.github.io/rock-paper-scissors/) · [On itch.io](https://sparklebeard.itch.io/ro-sham-bo-exe) · [Source](https://github.com/anthony-liddle/rock-paper-scissors)

## The Pitch

You land on a green-on-black terminal. You play a round of rock-paper-scissors. The robot says something unsettling. You play another round. Partway through, the robot asks for permission to send you notifications. A few rounds later it wants your location. Then your camera. Then your microphone. Then your fullscreen.

You can decline every permission. You can mute the music. You can leave the tab. The robot notices, and the game keeps going until someone reaches five wins, at which point one of two endings plays out, and the robot remembers you next time.

Underneath all of this, the opponent's move is `crypto.getRandomValues`. The game is provably fair. The adversarial feeling comes from a tension system that reshapes music, visuals, and dialogue in response to player behavior, not from any manipulation of the match.

I built this partly because I wanted to see what happens when a small game takes browser permissions seriously as a narrative device, and partly as an excuse to stitch together two other tools I'd made: `soundscape-engine` for the audio and `ascii-roto` for the animations.

## The Interesting Problem

The central design problem is simple to state and difficult to execute: make the player feel watched without actually watching them. Every violation the robot performs has to be a performance, not a real intrusion. The camera stream has to be live enough to trigger the indicator light. The pixels cannot be read. The pseudo-fingerprint the robot taunts you with has to sound specific, but it has to be computed locally and never leave the browser. The geolocation city injected into the ending dialogue has to land as "it really does know," but it can't be sent anywhere beyond a Nominatim reverse-geocode.

If any of those lines are crossed, the project becomes the thing it's pretending to be.

This is ultimately a constraint problem: creating the feeling of surveillance without introducing actual surveillance.

The second problem is making five tension tiers feel like a single coherent escalation rather than a collection of unrelated feature flags. The tension score is a number between 0 and 100. Everything downstream reads from it: music BPM, which audio layers are active, idle animation frame rate, dialogue pool selection, the page title, the favicon, whether the ASCII frames are being actively corrupted. The hard part wasn't any individual connection. The hard part was making each subsystem escalate at a pace that matched the others, so moving from UNEASY to IRRITATED feels like one shift, not seven.

The third problem is that a player who refuses every permission, mutes the music, and closes the tab halfway through has to still feel like they lost. The robot's response to refusal is not to give up. It's to get angrier.

## Key Decisions and Tradeoffs

**The opponent is cryptographically fair.** Move selection is `crypto.getRandomValues(new Uint32Array(1))[0] % 3`. Not `Math.random`, not a weighted distribution, not reactive to sensor data. Everywhere else, `Math.random` is used for visual effects, dialogue selection, and frame corruption. The asymmetry is deliberate and it matters: the game is architecturally honest about the one thing the player would care most about (whether the match is rigged) and theatrical about everything else. If a player reverse-engineers the source, they find a fair RNG and a completely performative everything else. That's the joke.

**Permission streams are acquired and held, not read.** `navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia({ video: true })` runs when tension hits 60. The returned `MediaStream` is stored in a module-level holder so it doesn't get garbage-collected, which keeps the browser's camera indicator active. No `ImageCapture`, no `<video>` element, no canvas sampling. Same for the microphone at tension 70. The indicator light is the payload. This was the central design decision of the whole project: the unease has to come from the player's awareness that they've granted access, not from anything the access is being used for.

**Refusal is fuel.** Every permission denial adds +3 to the tension spike. A player who clicks "Block" on all five permissions accelerates their own escalation. Combined with the tab-leave detector (+2 per leave, capped at three), the mute detector (+3), the DevTools detector (+5 bonus and specific console taunting), and the abandonment count stored in `localStorage` (+5 per previous session that ended without a conclusion), there is no defensive way to play this game. Every form of opting out is visible to the system and becomes part of the system's response. I considered making denial neutral, as a "we respect your choice" signal. The game works better when it doesn't.

**The fingerprint is real, the surveillance is fake.** `illusionEngine.ts` computes a SHA-256 hash of `userAgent | screen dimensions | timezone | language | hardwareConcurrency` using `crypto.subtle.digest`. It reads the Battery Status API on browsers that still support it, `navigator.deviceMemory`, and `Intl.DateTimeFormat`. It uses all of this to select dialogue that name-drops the player's specific machine details. The probability of that dialogue firing scales from 0% at CALM to 50% at MELTDOWN. Everything stays on the device. The surveillance is the illusion of surveillance, built from real data the browser was already willing to share. That distinction is the core thesis.

**The console is a second screen.** `consoleNarrator.ts` treats DevTools as game surface. It prints styled color-coded messages synchronized to game events, floods the console at 1500-3500ms intervals during MELTDOWN, and reacts specifically if the DevTools panel is detected as open (measured by a 160px outer/inner width delta, polled every two seconds). Most players will never see any of this. The players who do get a different, more personalized version of the game. I considered whether building for an audience most players will never encounter was worth it. For this specific project, yes: the kind of player who opens DevTools on a game titled RO-SHAM-BO.EXE is exactly who the game is written for.

**Tension is a single scalar, state is distributed.** `tensionScore` lives in a hand-rolled pub-sub store consumed via `useSyncExternalStore`. Every subsystem reads it independently: `musicManager` changes BPM and the mixer state, `AsciiAnimation` changes frame rate and turns on corruption, `useBrowserChrome` updates the title and favicon, `dialogueSelector` reshuffles the pool. No reducer, no Redux, no Zustand, no context. A store with setters and subscribers, ~450 lines. The game runs on one number and several independent consumers of that number. The fact that it works without cross-coupling is intentional.

**Memory persists across sessions.** `playerMemory.ts` writes to `localStorage` under `roshambo-memory`: play count, last ending (`BROKEN` or `ESCAPED`), granted and denied permission arrays, the geolocated city if it was ever captured, last played date, abandonment count. On the next session, initial tension is seeded higher for returning players. A previous ESCAPED ending adds +40 to the starting tension, a previous BROKEN adds +20, each abandonment adds +5 up to a cap of +20. Returning players skip the polite early dialogue entirely. The robot also injects lines that reference their previous outcome. This is the decision that shifts the game from a toy into a piece.

**Six audio layers driven by tension, plus per-event SFX.** The `soundscape-engine` integration is different from my other projects. Instead of a single composition that plays through once, the music is a 16-beat loop with seven precomposed tracks plus one procedurally generated `glitch-random` track built fresh each session. As tension rises, `setTempo` steps from 60 BPM at CALM to 150 BPM at MELTDOWN, and `updateMixer` progressively unmutes layers: bass at UNEASY, pad at IRRITATED, lead at UNSTABLE, both glitch tracks at MELTDOWN. On top of the loop, `previewNote` fires event-triggered one-shots: a win stinger, a low dissonant lose cluster, a permission-request delay-effect tone, harsh disruption bursts, and two distinct ending stingers (descending rumble for BROKEN, ascending ominous tones for ESCAPED). The same library that plays a single 60-second piece in The Forgetting Machine runs a live dynamic mixer here. That range was the point of making it framework-free.

**31 ASCII animations driving 800+ frames.** All animations are filmed source video converted to ASCII frames via my own `ascii-roto` tool, then exported as per-animation JSON arrays and committed to `/public/animations`. A frame is rendered by writing the string to a `<pre>` element's `textContent` in VT323 monospace. Idle animations ping-pong; choice animations play forward; ending animations play forward and hold. Frame rate is tension-dependent, from 12 fps at CALM to 36 fps at MELTDOWN. At MELTDOWN, frames are corrupted on the fly: 3-8% of non-space characters replaced with glitch glyphs, contiguous rows occasionally swapped with rows from a different frame, rows occasionally duplicated. The corruption is deterministic noise on top of an otherwise stable loop, which is why it reads as system degradation rather than random artifacting.

## Stack

React 19 with TypeScript, Vite, Vitest. Four production dependencies: `react`, `react-dom`, `soundscape-engine`, `posthog-js` (the analytics is disabled when the browser sends Do Not Track). Hand-rolled state store via `useSyncExternalStore`, no state library. Pre-commit hook runs full TypeScript build, ESLint, and the Vitest suite before allowing a commit. TypeScript configured strict with `noUncheckedIndexedAccess`, `erasableSyntaxOnly`, and `verbatimModuleSyntax`. Deployed to two surfaces: as a web build on GitHub Pages, and as an HTML5 build on itch.io under the handle `sparklebeard`.

## The Pipeline

This project sits downstream of two others. `soundscape-engine` provides the audio library; all of the music in RO-SHAM-BO.EXE is a composition I wrote in my own soundscape composer.

The ASCII animations came from a different tool: `ascii-roto`, a CLI I built for converting video to ASCII. Every animation started as filmed video, processed through `ascii-roto`, and committed as a per-animation module. The tool began as a prototype I ran over and over against whatever file I was working on at the moment. When I decided it was useful enough to be a real project, I promoted it to a proper CLI, kept the output format it had been emitting all along, and pointed the game at it. The downstream use shaped the tool, not the other way around.

The game is the payoff of a chain of three tools, and it's the place where all of them land in service of a single bit. Most portfolios show discrete projects. This one lets earlier work earn its keep.

## Numbers

- About 8,542 lines of TypeScript across 61 source files. Largest single file: `dialogue.ts` at 600+ lines of written copy.
- Five tension tiers, five permission escalation steps, five wins to end the game.
- Opponent move: `crypto.getRandomValues`. No weighting, no memory, no player-dependent behavior.
- Music: one 16-beat loop, seven precomposed tracks plus one procedurally generated, tempo from 60 to 150 BPM across tiers. Eight distinct SFX events fired via `previewNote`.
- 31 ASCII animations, 800+ total frames. Frame rate scales from 12 fps at CALM to 36 fps at MELTDOWN. Choice animations play at 55 fps, endings at 65.
- Permission thresholds: notification at 20, geolocation at 40, camera at 60, microphone at 70, fullscreen at 80. Each denial adds +3 tension spike.
- Returning players start with tension seeded by prior outcomes: +40 for a previous ESCAPED ending, +20 for BROKEN, +5 per abandonment up to +20.
- DevTools detection polls every 2 seconds at a 160px outer/inner width threshold. Console narrator floods at 1500-3500ms intervals during MELTDOWN.
- 8 test files, all covering the pure-logic subsystems (resolver, tension, memory, random, frame corruption). Behavior-heavy subsystems (illusion engine, console narrator, music manager) are untested.

## What I'd Do Differently

The behavior-heavy subsystems are untested. The pure-logic files have proper coverage but the illusion engine, the console narrator, and the music manager are all held together by manual play-testing. The premise is that the system reacts to the player in legible ways. The absence of tests for that reaction layer is the weakest part of the repo.

There's no single onboarding screen for permissions. Each one is introduced contextually via dialogue, which works for tone, but means a player who declines early permissions is given less information than one who accepts. A short, in-character pre-permissions explainer would hold the bit and give keyboard users a proper focus target. Speaking of which: the permission prompt is keyboard-addressable (Y or Enter to accept, N or Escape to decline) but has no explicit ARIA roles or focus management. That would be the first thing I'd fix.

The opponent never actually gets smarter, even though the robot claims it's learning your patterns. That decision was deliberate, as the game is about the illusion of surveillance, not the reality. However, if I revisited it I'd consider a single, subtle adaptation: let the robot bias slightly against your most-played move when tension is high. Not enough to change the outcome distribution meaningfully over a five-win match, but enough that a player studying the source would find something real underneath the taunts.

The README in the `/public/animations` folder says nothing about where the frames originated. A short note explaining the `ascii-roto` pipeline would save the case study from having to reconstruct it.

## What's Next?

Probably nothing major at this point. The game is complete. There are two small TODOs in the code (confirmation copy, scoreboard spacing) and a couple of dev-only pages I never linked from the main game (animation browser, audio mixer, memory reset). The remaining work is polish, not features.

The project I'd most want to revisit is not RPS itself but the `ascii-roto` pipeline that fed it. Right now the pipeline works but it's manual: I film, I run the tool, I export, I commit. A tighter loop, possibly as a build-time plugin, would make future projects using ASCII animation faster to bootstrap.
